Chapter 13 Bankruptcy Mills in Tampa:
Red Flags and What to Watch For

Chapter 13 is where bankruptcy mills do the most damage. A 3-5 year repayment plan means years of payments, and if the case is dismissed, you walk away with nothing. Here is what to watch for in Tampa.

Why Chapter 13 Is Where Mills Thrive

Chapter 13 bankruptcy requires a debtor to make monthly payments over 3 to 5 years. Attorney fees are typically paid through the plan, meaning the attorney gets paid even if the case is ultimately dismissed. This fee structure creates a perverse incentive: the attorney profits from filing the case, regardless of whether the client ever receives a discharge.

Tampa's Chapter 13 market, like Miami's, features significant attorney concentration. The Middle District of Florida's multiple divisions create local sub-markets where a small number of firms dominate consumer filings. Dismissal rates across the district vary by division, but the Tampa division has drawn particular attention for high-volume practices.

The "filed to fail" pattern: When an attorney files a Chapter 13 plan that is unlikely to succeed, the debtor still pays attorney fees, the attorney still earns their fee, and the client bears the full cost of failure. Identifying this pattern requires comparing individual attorney dismissal rates against the court average.

Chapter 13 Red Flags in Tampa

Based on patterns observed across federal bankruptcy courts, here are specific warning signs that your Tampa Chapter 13 attorney may be operating as a mill:

1. You never met with the attorney before filing

If your initial consultation was entirely with a paralegal or intake coordinator, and the attorney's first involvement was signing the petition, that is a red flag. The attorney has a duty to review your financial situation personally and ensure the filing is appropriate.

2. Your plan was filed with no discussion of terms

A Chapter 13 plan should reflect your specific financial situation -- your income, expenses, debts, and goals. If you were never asked about your monthly expenses, your vehicle needs, your income stability, or your long-term financial goals, the plan may be a template rather than a tailored document.

3. Monthly Operating Reports are late or missing

In some cases, Monthly Operating Reports (MORs) are required. Chronic failure to file MORs is a strong indicator of attorney disengagement. Ask your attorney whether your case requires MORs and whether they are current.

4. You cannot reach your attorney

If your calls go to voicemail, your emails go unanswered for days, and your only contact is with staff members who cannot answer substantive legal questions, your case may not be receiving adequate attention. You have a right to communicate with your attorney.

5. Your attorney also handles hundreds of other active cases

There is no specific case limit in Florida ethics rules, but an attorney handling 200+ active Chapter 13 cases while also maintaining new case intake may not have the bandwidth for meaningful individual attention. Ask how many active cases your attorney personally manages.

Document everything. If you observe these patterns, keep a record of every missed communication, every late filing, and every instance where your case was not handled as promised. This documentation is essential if you later file a complaint with the Florida Bar or seek a fee refund.

What to Do Before Filing Chapter 13 in Tampa

Ask about Chapter 7 first. Many filers who end up in Chapter 13 would have been better served by Chapter 7, which provides a faster discharge with no multi-year payment plan. Use the means test calculator to check your eligibility.

Check your discharge eligibility. Under 11 U.S.C. § 1328(f), prior filers face waiting periods before they can receive a Chapter 13 discharge. An attorney who files a case for a client ineligible for discharge is not performing adequate due diligence.

Get a second opinion. If you have already been told that Chapter 13 is your only option, consider consulting with a different attorney -- or with Bay Area Legal Services -- to confirm that assessment.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is the Chapter 13 success rate in Tampa?
Chapter 13 success rates vary by district and by attorney. Nationally, roughly 33-40% of Chapter 13 cases result in a successful discharge. In the Middle District of Florida (M.D. Fla.), outcomes depend heavily on which attorney handles the case. Some high-volume practitioners have dismissal rates well above the court average, while others maintain rates below it.
How much does Chapter 13 cost in Tampa?
Chapter 13 attorney fees in Tampa typically range from $3,000 to $5,500, paid through the bankruptcy plan over 3-5 years. The court filing fee is $313. Total out-of-pocket depends on your plan terms. Importantly, attorney fees are often paid even when the case is dismissed -- meaning the attorney earns their fee regardless of whether you receive a discharge.
Why are Chapter 13 cases dismissed in Tampa?
Common causes include failed plan payments, failure to file required documents, and failure to pay the filing fee. Plan failures can result from genuinely changed circumstances, but they can also result from poorly designed plans that were never realistic. When an attorney files a plan unlikely to succeed, the debtor bears the full cost of failure.
How long does Chapter 13 take in Tampa?
A Chapter 13 plan runs 3 to 5 years. Below-median-income debtors may qualify for a 3-year plan; above-median-income debtors must commit to 5 years. If the case is dismissed before completion, the debtor receives no discharge and may have spent years making payments with no debt relief.
Can I switch from Chapter 13 to Chapter 7 in Tampa?
Yes, conversion from Chapter 13 to Chapter 7 is generally available under 11 U.S.C. § 1307(a) as a matter of right, provided the case was not previously converted from Chapter 7. This can be important if your circumstances change. However, Chapter 7 has its own eligibility requirements including the means test.
