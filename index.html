Tampa Bankruptcy Mill:
Warning Signs and What the Court Data Shows

An independent, data-driven guide to identifying bankruptcy mill practices in the Tampa metropolitan area. Based on public federal court records from the Middle District of Florida.

What Is a Bankruptcy Mill?

A bankruptcy mill is a high-volume law firm that processes large numbers of bankruptcy cases with minimal individual attorney attention. The term is not a formal legal designation -- it describes a pattern of practice. Mill attorneys typically handle hundreds of active cases simultaneously, delegate most client contact to non-attorney staff, use template documents with minimal customization, and earn their fees regardless of whether the client's case succeeds or fails.

The Middle District of Florida is one of the geographically largest bankruptcy districts in the country, covering Tampa, Orlando, Jacksonville, and Fort Myers. The district's consumer bankruptcy docket is driven by Florida's combination of high housing costs, lack of state income tax, and a large population of retirees and service-industry workers.

Why this matters in Tampa: High filing volumes across the I-4 corridor with documented patterns of attorney concentration in Tampa, Orlando, and Jacksonville divisions.

The Tampa Bankruptcy Landscape

Bankruptcy cases in the Tampa area are filed in the Middle District of Florida (M.D. Fla.). This federal court handles all Chapter 7 and Chapter 13 consumer bankruptcy cases for the district. Understanding the local landscape is essential for anyone considering filing -- or wondering whether their current attorney is providing adequate representation.

Tampa's Chapter 13 market, like Miami's, features significant attorney concentration. The Middle District of Florida's multiple divisions create local sub-markets where a small number of firms dominate consumer filings. Dismissal rates across the district vary by division, but the Tampa division has drawn particular attention for high-volume practices.

Key Questions for Tampa Filers

1. What is your dismissal rate?

Any attorney should be able to tell you what percentage of their Chapter 13 cases are dismissed. If they don't know or won't say, that is itself a signal.

2. How many active cases do you personally handle?

An attorney handling 200+ active Chapter 13 cases may not have bandwidth for meaningful individual attention. Ask who will actually work on your case day-to-day.

3. Have you checked whether I'm eligible for a discharge?

Under 11 U.S.C. § 1328(f), prior filers face waiting periods. This is a threshold question that should be answered before any filing.

4. Would Chapter 7 be a better fit for my situation?

Chapter 13 plans run 3-5 years with significant failure risk. If you qualify for Chapter 7, it may be the faster, safer option. Ask your attorney to explain why they recommend one chapter over the other.

5. How are your fees structured?

In Chapter 13, attorney fees are typically paid through the plan. Ask for the total fee upfront and compare it to the court-approved fee guidelines for the M.D. Fla..

How to Protect Yourself

Before hiring a bankruptcy attorney in Tampa, take these steps to evaluate the quality of representation you will receive:

Check PACER records. The Public Access to Court Electronic Records system allows you to search for any attorney's bankruptcy case history in the M.D. Fla.. Look for patterns: high dismissal rates, repeated case failures, or a volume of active cases that suggests inadequate individual attention.

Verify bar standing. The Florida Bar maintains public records of attorney licensing and disciplinary history. An attorney with a history of professional conduct complaints deserves additional scrutiny.

Check discharge eligibility. Before filing, use the free discharge eligibility screener to confirm that you are eligible for a discharge under 11 U.S.C. § 1328(f). An attorney who files a case for a client who cannot receive a discharge is, at minimum, not performing adequate due diligence.

Consider free legal aid. Bay Area Legal Services provides free legal assistance to qualifying residents. If you cannot afford an attorney, a legal aid organization may be a better option than a mill.

Not all high-volume attorneys are mills. Volume alone does not make a mill. Some high-volume attorneys achieve excellent outcomes through efficient systems. The key metric is outcomes: what percentage of cases result in a successful discharge versus dismissal.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is a bankruptcy mill?
A bankruptcy mill is a high-volume law firm that processes large numbers of bankruptcy cases with minimal individual attention. These firms often have dismissal rates significantly above the court average, delegate most work to non-attorney staff, and profit regardless of whether the client's case succeeds. The term has no formal legal definition, but the pattern is well-documented in federal court data and recognized by judges, trustees, and bar associations.
How do I know if my Tampa bankruptcy attorney is a mill?
Warning signs include: difficulty reaching your actual attorney, most communication through paralegals or assistants, missed filing deadlines, cookie-cutter plan terms without discussion of your specific situation, and a pattern of cases being dismissed. You can check dismissal rates and filing volumes through public court records at PACER. The Florida Bar can also tell you whether an attorney has any disciplinary history.
What court handles bankruptcy cases in Tampa?
Bankruptcy cases in the Tampa metropolitan area are filed in the Middle District of Florida (M.D. Fla.). This is a federal court -- bankruptcy is exclusively a federal matter under Title 11 of the United States Code. The court's public records are available through PACER (Public Access to Court Electronic Records).
How can I check a Tampa bankruptcy attorney's track record?
Public court records through PACER contain the outcome of every bankruptcy case. You can search by attorney name to see their full case history, including how many cases were dismissed versus discharged. The 1328f.com discharge screener provides district-level data. The Florida Bar can confirm licensing and disciplinary history.
What should I do if I think my Tampa attorney is a mill?
If you believe your attorney is not providing adequate representation: (1) document specific failures -- missed deadlines, unanswered communications, filing errors; (2) request a copy of your complete case file; (3) consult with another bankruptcy attorney for a second opinion; (4) file a complaint with the Florida Bar if warranted; (5) consider filing a motion to substitute counsel with the court. You have the right to competent representation.
Where can I get free legal help in Tampa?
Bay Area Legal Services provides free legal assistance to qualifying residents. The U.S. Trustee's office (Region 21) also maintains resources for debtors. You can also search for pro bono bankruptcy attorneys through the LawHelp.org directory.
