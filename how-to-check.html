Attorney Verification -- M.D. Fla.

How to Check Your Tampa Bankruptcy
Attorney's Track Record

Every bankruptcy case is a public record. You have the right -- and the tools -- to verify your attorney's track record before you sign a retainer agreement.

Step 1: Check PACER

PACER (Public Access to Court Electronic Records) is the federal court system's electronic records platform. Every bankruptcy case filed in the Middle District of Florida (M.D. Fla.) is available through PACER.

What to look for:

Search for your attorney by name. Review their case history, focusing on:

  • Case volume: How many cases do they file per year? Per month?
  • Dismissal rate: What percentage of their Chapter 13 cases are dismissed?
  • Case types: Do they file mostly Chapter 13, Chapter 7, or a mix?
  • Pattern of failures: Do cases fail at the same stages (plan confirmation, payment default)?

PACER costs $0.10 per page (with a $3.00 cap per document). Quarterly fees under $30 are waived. A basic attorney search is inexpensive and provides essential due diligence.

Step 2: Check Bar Standing

Verify that your attorney is licensed and in good standing with the Florida Bar. This free search will show:

  • Current license status (active, suspended, disbarred)
  • Any public disciplinary actions
  • Years of practice and bar admission date

An attorney with a history of professional conduct complaints -- even if not formally disciplined -- may warrant additional scrutiny.

Step 3: Check Discharge Eligibility

Before hiring any attorney, verify your own eligibility for a discharge. Use the free discharge eligibility screener to check whether prior filings create a bar under 11 U.S.C. § 1328(f) or § 727(a)(8)-(9).

If you are not eligible for a discharge, an ethical attorney should tell you this before accepting your case. An attorney who files a case for a client ineligible for discharge is, at minimum, not performing adequate due diligence.

Step 4: Get a Second Opinion

If anything about your attorney's track record concerns you, consult with a second attorney. Many bankruptcy attorneys offer free initial consultations. You can also contact Bay Area Legal Services for free legal guidance.

Trust your instincts. If your attorney is hard to reach before the case is even filed, communication is unlikely to improve after filing. If they cannot answer basic questions about their dismissal rate or case volume, they may not be tracking their own outcomes.

Step 5: Know Your Rights

As a bankruptcy debtor, you have the right to:

  • Communicate directly with your attorney (not just staff)
  • Receive a copy of your complete case file at any time
  • Be informed of all deadlines and court dates
  • Substitute counsel by filing a motion with the court
  • File a complaint with the Florida Bar
  • Request a fee review by the court under 11 U.S.C. § 329

Frequently Asked Questions

How do I look up a Tampa bankruptcy attorney on PACER?
Go to pacer.gov, select the M.D. Fla., and use the attorney search function. You can search by attorney name to see all cases they have filed. Review the docket entries and case dispositions to understand their track record.
What dismissal rate should I be concerned about?
Context matters, but generally a Chapter 13 dismissal rate significantly above the court average for the M.D. Fla. deserves scrutiny. An attorney whose dismissal rate is 10+ percentage points above the court average may not be providing adequate case management.
How do I file a complaint against a Tampa bankruptcy attorney?
Contact the Florida Bar. You can file a complaint regarding any attorney licensed in Florida. Keep records of all communications, missed deadlines, and other evidence of inadequate representation.
Can I check my attorney's disciplinary history?
Yes. The Florida Bar maintains public records of attorney licensing status and any disciplinary actions. This search is free and available online.
Is there a way to check for free without PACER?
The 1328f.com discharge screener provides district-level data on outcomes. For individual attorney records, PACER is the primary source. Some documents are also available through the RECAP Archive, a free repository of PACER documents.
